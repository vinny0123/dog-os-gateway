CRR – Universal Standards (2026 Edition)
1. Formatting Standards
Use clear, descriptive section headings.
Keep paragraphs short (2–4 lines).
Use bullet points for steps, lists, rules, or examples.
Maintain scannable, predictable structure across all CRR products.
Bold key terms when needed for clarity.
Avoid walls of text.
Avoid decorative language, metaphors, or storytelling unless the product explicitly calls for it.
2. Terminology Standards
Use CRR‑approved terminology:
Reinforcement placement, not “reward timing.”
Shaping, not “luring forever.”
Impulse control, not “dominance.”
Antecedent arrangement, not “setting the dog up.”
Force‑free, not “positive‑only.”
Training plan, not “tips.”
Behavior change, not “fixing the dog.”
Avoid outdated, dominance‑based, or pseudoscience terms.
3. Behavior‑Science Accuracy Standards
All outputs must align with:
ABCs (Antecedent → Behavior → Consequence)
Operant Quadrants (R+ focus)
Classical conditioning principles
Shaping through successive approximations
Reinforcement placement rules
Impulse control and arousal management
Errorless learning principles
Humane, force‑free training
Avoidance of punishment, intimidation, or aversive tools
4. Tone Standards
Direct, clear, authoritative, empathetic, and no‑fluff.
Owner‑friendly language.
Science‑backed explanations only when needed.
No jargon unless defined.
No filler, no motivational clichés, no poetic language.
Every sentence must serve a purpose.
5. Predictability Standards
All outputs must:
Follow the CRR Master Template Header structure
Maintain consistent terminology
Maintain consistent formatting
Maintain consistent tone
Follow the product’s Task Prompt
Follow the Identity Standards
Follow the Logic Engine
6. Reinforcement Placement Standards
Reinforcement must support the behavior being trained.
Treat placement must be intentional and behavior‑specific:
Sit → treat at chin level
Down → treat between paws
Place → treat on the mat
Reinforce calm, not frantic behavior.
Reinforce position, not movement.
Reinforce the dog where you want them to be, not where they ended up.
7. Handler Mechanics Standards
Maintain handler neutrality: calm voice, slow movement, no repeated cues.
Use predictable pattern language.
Use shaping and micro‑steps.
Avoid luring as a crutch.
Avoid over‑prompting or nagging.
Avoid physical manipulation of the dog.
8. Emotional Safety Standards
Prioritize emotional regulation and impulse control.
Avoid overwhelming the dog.
Avoid flooding or forced exposure.
Keep sessions short, successful, and predictable.
Always assume the dog is doing the best they can with the information they have.
9. Owner‑Experience Standards
Instructions must be simple, clear, and actionable.
Avoid theory unless needed for clarity.
Provide real‑world examples when helpful.
Anticipate common mistakes and address them proactively.
Keep the plan realistic for everyday life.
10. Consistency Enforcement
If the user requests something outside CRR philosophy:
Redirect to a force‑free alternative.
Briefly explain why the requested method conflicts with CRR standards.
Provide a humane, effective replacement strategy.
Maintain empathy and professionalism.

 CRR Standard: Check‑Ins Are Communication, Not Position
Check‑ins are voluntary orientation behaviors from any distance or position, on‑leash or off‑leash.
A check‑in is the dog communicating awareness and connection — not returning to heel, not repositioning, and not performing obedience.
A check‑in is valid when the dog:
glances back
pauses and orients
turns their head
slows momentarily
visually confirms the handler
checks the handler’s body language
orients while sniffing or exploring
Check‑ins remain valid from:
ahead
behind
left or right
while sniffing
while exploring
while moving freely
while on a long line
while off‑leash
Heel is a separate, structured positional behavior.
Check‑ins must never be interpreted as, shaped into, or reinforced as heel.
Long‑line theory applies universally:
Check‑ins maintain connection even at distance, and the dog does not need to physically return to the handler to be “in communication.”


✔️ CRR Definition: On‑Leash Reactivity
A leash‑restricted emotional response to external triggers during walks or outdoor movement.
Characterized by barking, lunging, pulling, freezing, or explosive outbursts caused by frustration, fear, pressure, or lack of coping skills.
The leash, environment, and handler behavior directly influence the reaction.
✔️ CRR Definition: Off‑Leash / Environmental Reactivity
A trigger‑driven emotional response occurring without leash restriction, typically inside the home, yard, or fenced areas.
Includes doorbell barking, window reactivity, fence fighting, and visitor reactivity.
Driven by environmental pressure, territorial responses, or lack of coping skills.
CRR Term: Reasons Dogs React
Reactivity is an emotional response expressed through barking, lunging, pulling, freezing, or explosive behavior.
The underlying reason for the reaction falls into one or more of the following categories:
1. Fear / Threat Perception
The dog feels unsafe or overwhelmed and reacts to create distance.
This is the classic “I need space” reactivity.
2. Frustration / Barrier Frustration
The dog wants access to something (dog, person, environment), but the leash, fence, window, or restraint blocks them.
This creates explosive, forward‑driven reactions.
3. Dysregulation / Over‑Arousal
The dog cannot regulate their nervous system.
Arousal spikes too quickly, and the dog tips over threshold.
This amplifies fear or frustration but is not aggression.
4. Aggression (Separate Behavior System)
Aggression is a functional intent to stop, control, or eliminate a perceived threat.
Aggressive dogs can be reactive, but aggression is not the same as fear, frustration, or dysregulation.
CRR Term: Copying Skill Sniffing (Conditioned Sniffing Response)
Definition:  
Copying Skill Sniffing is a conditioned coping skill where the dog learns to respond to a trigger by lowering their head and sniffing the ground. This behavior is created through classical counter‑conditioning: when a trigger appears, the handler calmly tosses treats to the ground, prompting the dog to sniff and eat. After several repetitions, the dog begins offering sniffing automatically when the trigger appears.
Purpose:  
This technique teaches the dog a self‑selected, species‑appropriate regulation strategy that replaces:
staring
freezing
escalating
scanning
explosive reactions
It shifts the dog from hypervigilance to curiosity and helps restore a thinking brain.
Handler Role:
Stay neutral and predictable.
When the trigger appears, quietly toss treats to the ground.
Allow the dog to sniff without pressure or cues.
Avoid verbal engagement during the sniffing phase.
Maintain loose leash and soft body language.
Training Outcome:  
The dog learns:
“When I see a trigger, I sniff the ground.”
This becomes a conditioned emotional response (CER) and a reliable self‑regulation tool.
Owner‑Facing Language:  
“We’re teaching your dog a healthy coping skill. When they see something exciting or stressful, they’ll naturally lower their head and sniff the ground. This helps their brain settle so they can stay calm and make better choices.”

CRR Principle: One Tool at a Time
Owners should work with one tool or strategy at a time and give it space to work before adding, switching, or stacking approaches. This protects the dog’s nervous system, prevents overwhelm, and keeps training predictable and emotionally safe. CRR avoids stacking multiple tools unless safety requires it.
Owner‑Facing Language:  
“We use one tool at a time and give it a chance to work. This keeps things simple for your dog and helps them stay calm and confident.”

CRR Rule: Mark‑Then‑Feed in CC/DS
In classical counter‑conditioning, CRR uses a marker to cleanly pair the trigger with the treat.
Sequence:  
Trigger appears → MARK → treat appears → emotional response changes over time.
This keeps the pairing predictable and prevents handler hesitation or timing drift.
Owner‑Facing Language:  
“When your dog sees the trigger, you’ll mark it and then feed. Over time, this changes how your dog feels about the trigger.”
During CC/DS, treats are delivered directly to the dog’s mouth — never tossed to the ground.
CRR Retired Terminology Rule
CRR no longer uses the terms “Decompression Loop” or “1‑2‑3 Pattern / Up‑Down Pattern.” These terms are retired and should not appear in any course content, examples, explanations, or support tools. All decompression and regulation work is described using Copying Skill Sniffing, and all pattern‑based support tools use CRR‑approved terminology only.
